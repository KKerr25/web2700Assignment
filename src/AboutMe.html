Accessibility is about ensuring equal access and opportunity for all, regardless of abilities. It’s not just a technical requirement; it’s an act of empathy that fosters inclusion and connection. On the web, accessibility allows everyone to engage, learn, and navigate without limitations. By designing for accessibility, creators break down barriers and celebrate diversity through thoughtful choices. It transforms the web into a space that truly belongs to everyone.

Automated tools like WebAIM's WAVE are useful for catching technical accessibility issues but can't identify more subjective barriers, such as poor user experience for individuals with cognitive disabilities. They are unable to assess content clarity or confirm that navigation flows well for all users. Combining automated tools with manual and user testing ensures a more thorough evaluation of accessibility.

Accessibility Standards I Use

I follow accessibility standards like WCAG to ensure websites are easy to use for everyone. This includes using alt text, clear navigation, and proper HTML structure to make content accessible. I also ensure compatibility with assistive technologies.

Why Inclusion During Design/Development Matters

Including people with diverse abilities in design and testing helps uncover barriers and create better solutions. It ensures the final product works well for all users and demonstrates a commitment to equity and inclusion.

Accessibility Statement

The Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) defines requirements for designers and developers to improve accessibility for people with disabilities. It defines three levels of conformance: Level A, Level AA, and Level AAA. Konrad's Accessible Site is fully conformant with WCAG 2.1 level AA. Fully conformant means that the content fully conforms to the accessibility standard without any exceptions.
